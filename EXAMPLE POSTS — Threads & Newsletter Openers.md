### THREADS POST 1 — Mistake → Fix (Listicle)

**Content Pillar:** Mistake → Fix | **Hook Type:** ALL-CAPS Ultimatum

---

STOP buying gear to fix a creativity problem.

I upgraded my camera three times in two years.

My photos got sharper.
They didn't get better.

Because sharp ≠ interesting.
And better gear just makes bad habits more expensive.

Here's what actually moved the needle:

→ Stopped shooting everything. Started shooting *one thing* obsessively.
→ Deleted "safe" shots the same day I took them.
→ Swapped YouTube rabbit holes for one intentional hour outside.

The camera you have is not the ceiling.

Your eye is the ceiling — and that one's free to raise.

Pick one of these this week. Just one. See what shifts.

Film presets don't give you a film look.

### THREADS POST 3 — Story Thread (Narrative / Before-After)

**Content Pillar:** Story Thread | **Hook Type:** Contrast Flashback

---

Me at 21: terrified of manual mode. Shot full auto and prayed.

Me at 28: manual mode is the only thing that makes sense.

Here's what changed — and it wasn't practice.

It was *understanding*.

The exposure triangle isn't a technical concept.
It's a creative conversation:

- **Aperture** asks: how much of this world do I want in focus?
- **Shutter speed** asks: do I want to freeze this or let it breathe?
- **ISO** asks: how much grain am I willing to carry for this shot?

Three questions. Every frame.

Once I started thinking like that, manual mode wasn't scary.
It was just storytelling with extra steps.

What's the thing about manual mode that's still tripping you up? Comment it. I'll fix it. 👇

### NEWSLETTER OPENER 1 — Confession / Vulnerability Hook

**Subject line:** I almost quit after someone called my work "try-hard"

---

Hey,

I need to tell you something I've never put in writing.

About four years into shooting seriously, I posted what I thought was my best photo ever. Golden light, perfect exposure, intentional composition — the whole deal.

One comment. Buried in the thread.

*"Classic try-hard. All technique, no soul."*

I closed my laptop and didn't pick up my camera for three weeks.

Here's the part I didn't understand then: they were right.

Not about quitting — but about the photo. It *was* technically proficient and emotionally empty. I'd been so obsessed with getting the settings correct that I'd completely forgotten to feel anything before pressing the shutter.

That comment broke something in me.

Then it built something better.

This week I want to share the exact shift that happened after those three weeks away — and the single question I now ask before every single frame I take.

It's not a technical question. And it won't cost you a new lens to use it.

Let's get into it.

— Glenn

### NEWSLETTER OPENER 2 — Contrarian / Myth Bust Hook

**Subject line:** The editing advice everyone gives is backwards

---

Hey,

Here's something the photography YouTube algorithm does not want you to hear:

Your editing style doesn't matter nearly as much as you think.

I know. I know. You've spent hours watching colour grading tutorials. You've bought LUT packs. You've reverse-engineered the tones on your three favourite photographers' feeds.

And your photos still feel... off.

Because here's the thing nobody says in those tutorials:

**Editing can only amplify what's already there.**

A beautifully graded photo of a boring moment is still a boring moment. A rushed, cluttered composition with a gorgeous teal-and-orange colour grade is still a rushed, cluttered composition.

The obsession with editing style is the photography world's version of buying a gym membership and spending all your time in the supplement aisle.

This week I'm walking you through the thing that actually comes first — before you even raise the camera — and why mastering it will do more for your photos than any preset pack ever could.

This one's free. And it'll probably make you delete a few presets.

— Glenn

### NEWSLETTER OPENER 3 — Journey / Transformation Hook

**Subject line:** The $0 upgrade that changed everything for me

---

Hey,

Two years ago I ran an experiment.

I gave myself one rule for an entire month: I could only photograph within a five-minute walk of my apartment.

No golden-hour chases. No road trips. No waiting for "better" conditions.

Just my street. My car park. My corner shop.

Honestly? I hated the idea. I thought I'd run out of things to shoot by day four.

I didn't.

By day twelve, I was seeing things I'd walked past a hundred times and never actually *looked* at. By day twenty, I started making photos I was genuinely proud of — not because the subject matter was impressive, but because I'd finally slowed down enough to find the story in the ordinary.

That experiment is why I keep saying: **your location is not your limitation. Your attention is.**

The most underrated photography skill isn't lighting or composition or post-processing.

It's learning to be still long enough for a scene to reveal itself to you.

This week I want to show you a few frames from that month — and the dead-simple practice that'll help you find the same thing in your neighbourhood, your backyard, your commute.

No gear required. Just your eyes and twenty minutes.

Here we go.

— Glenn
